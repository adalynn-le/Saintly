Introduction

Trigonometry is the study of angles and their relations in triangles. It is really popular on the MC 10 and a fundamental branch of geometry.

Basics

Trigonometry is the fundamental branch of maths that deals with triangles, including the relationships between their side lengths and angles.

First, let’s observe a right angled triangle.

Here, we are trying to find the angle \(x\). Given this, we can denote the sides of the triangle with special terminology.

  • The longest side of the triangle is called the hypotenuse.
  • The side directly opposite to the angle x is called the opposite.
  • The side directly next to angle x (the remaining side) is called the adjacent.

Now, we will introduce trigonometric ratios. These will help allow us to calculate angle \(x\).

\(\sin(x)=\frac{\textup{opposite}}{\textup{hypotenuse}}\) \(\cos(x)=\frac{\textup{adjacent}}{\textup{hypotenuse}}\) \(\tan(x)=\frac{\textup{opposite}}{\textup{adjacent}}\)

Here, we are trying to find the angle of the triangle denoted by the ? question mark. We are given 2 values of the triangle. The 5 represents the opposite of the triangle, as this side is directly opposite to the angle we are trying to find. The 10 is the triangle’s hypotenuse.

As we know the opposite and hypotenuse values, we can use the sine function to calculate the missing angle.

Thus \(\sin(?)=\frac{5}{10}\)

When we are trying to find the angle of a right angled triangle given 2 side lengths, we use the arcsine function. The arcsine function is the inverse of the sine function, which will allow us to find the missing angle.

\(\textup{arcsin}(\frac{5}{10})=? \textup{or} \sin^{-1}(?)=\frac{5}{10}\)

Note here that \(\sin^{-1}=\textup{arcsin}\), not \(\frac{1}{\textup{sin}}\). This relation is modeled by \(\textup{cosecant}\), the inverse of sine. The inverse of cosine (\(\frac{1}{\cos}\)) is \(\textup{secant}\), and for tangent \(\textup{cotangent}\)

Plugging into a calculator gives \(x=30^\circ\)

Laws and Identities

Reciprocal & Quotient Identities
Reciprocal \[\csc(\theta) = \frac{1}{\sin(\theta)}, \quad \sec(\theta) = \frac{1}{\cos(\theta)}, \quad \cot(\theta) = \frac{1}{\tan(\theta)}\]
Quotient \[\tan(\theta) = \frac{\sin(\theta)}{\cos(\theta)}, \quad \cot(\theta) = \frac{\cos(\theta)}{\sin(\theta)}\]
Pythagorean Identities
Sine & Cosine \[\sin^2(\theta) + \cos^2(\theta) = 1\]
Tangent & Secant \[1 + \tan^2(\theta) = \sec^2(\theta)\]
Cotangent & Cosecant \[1 + \cot^2(\theta) = \csc^2(\theta)\]
Sum and Difference Formulas
Sine \[\sin(\alpha \pm \beta) = \sin(\alpha)\cos(\beta) \pm \cos(\alpha)\sin(\beta)\]
Cosine \[\cos(\alpha \pm \beta) = \cos(\alpha)\cos(\beta) \mp \sin(\alpha)\sin(\beta)\]
Tangent \[\tan(\alpha \pm \beta) = \frac{\tan(\alpha) \pm \tan(\beta)}{1 \mp \tan(\alpha)\tan(\beta)}\]
Double & Half-Angle Formulas
Double Angle \[\sin(2\theta) = 2\sin(\theta)\cos(\theta)\] \[\cos(2\theta) = \cos^2(\theta) - \sin^2(\theta) = 2\cos^2(\theta) - 1 = 1 - 2\sin^2(\theta)\] \[\tan(2\theta) = \frac{2\tan(\theta)}{1 - \tan^2(\theta)}\]
Half Angle \[\sin\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right) = \pm\sqrt{\frac{1 - \cos(\theta)}{2}}\] \[\cos\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right) = \pm\sqrt{\frac{1 + \cos(\theta)}{2}}\] \[\tan\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right) = \pm\sqrt{\frac{1 - \cos(\theta)}{1 + \cos(\theta)}} = \frac{1 - \cos(\theta)}{\sin(\theta)} = \frac{\sin(\theta)}{1 + \cos(\theta)}\]
Laws of Non-Right Triangles
Law of Sines \[\frac{a}{\sin(A)} = \frac{b}{\sin(B)} = \frac{c}{\sin(C)}\]
Law of Cosines \[a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc\cos(A)\] \[b^2 = a^2 + c^2 - 2ac\cos(B)\] \[c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab\cos(C)\]
Triangle Area \[\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}ab\sin(C) = \frac{1}{2}bc\sin(A) = \frac{1}{2}ac\sin(B)\]

Conclusion

Trigonometry is an essential tool for studying the relationships of shapes and triangles in the unit circle. It has many formulas to memorize, but these can be derived easily through basic knowledge.
